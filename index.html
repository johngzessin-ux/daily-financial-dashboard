Daily Financial Trends

July 17, 2026 · Morning research digest for FA conversations today

1. Private Investments

Insurers are leaning further into private credit, not pulling back. Marsh McLennan's 2026 Global Insurance Investment Survey (123 insurers, 24 countries, $4T+ in assets, fielded March-April 2026) found 57% of insurers globally plan to increase private credit exposure over the next 12-24 months — 65% among US insurers, 74% among Canadian insurers — even as the same respondents flagged tightening spreads and eroding illiquidity premiums as their top concern. Useful stat for advisors weighing private credit allocations inside insurance-linked or annuity products.
Source: Alternative Credit Investor — "More than half of insurers set to increase private credit exposure" (July 17, 2026)
Private credit's secondary market is heating up as investors seek an exit ramp. Trading platform Tradable facilitated $646M in private credit loan transactions from January-April 2026 alone — already double all of 2025's volume — as funds field more redemption requests and look for ways to trade normally-illiquid loans. A sign of real liquidity stress building beneath the asset class's headline growth.
Source: Briefs — "Private Credit Trading Jumps as Investors Seek Redemptions"
US venture capital deployed a record $412.7B in H1 2026 — but almost none of it is trickling down. That's 30% more than all of 2025, yet 86% of dollars went to AI deals and 91% went to rounds of $100M+. PitchBook's Kyle Stanford: "This market is split into two very distinct areas" — mega-cap AI names (SpaceX, Anthropic, OpenAI) versus everyone else struggling for attention. Relevant context for clients with venture/growth-equity exposure asking why "VC is booming" doesn't match what their fund is seeing.
Source: Fortune — Allie Garfinkle, "In 2026 so far, U.S. VCs have deployed a record-shattering $412.7 billion" (July 10, 2026)
Commercial real estate is recalibrating by asset type, not moving as one market. Older multifamily and industrial properties are holding pricing power while office and retail lag; data center demand continues to surge. Investors are increasingly selective, prioritizing high-quality assets over short-term yield chasing, while tariffs and labor shortages push construction costs higher industry-wide.
Source: Engineers and Architects of America — "July 2026 Commercial Real Estate Market Trends Revealed"

2. Equities

The chip selloff is deepening, not fading — now spreading to Asia. TSMC posted record Q2 results (revenue $40.2B, net profit up 77% to ~$22B) but raised full-year capex guidance to $60-64B (from $52-56B) and lifted its US investment commitment toward $265B. Instead of celebrating the beat, markets read the capex jump as a warning on AI-spending sustainability: TSMC shares fell 7.3% in Taipei, the VanEck Semiconductor ETF (SMH) is down nearly 7% on the week, and the sector-wide gauge is off 20% from its record — its worst stretch since the April 2025 tariff meltdown.
Source: Bloomberg — "Chip Stock Rout Deepens as TSMC Selloff Drives Taiwan Correction" (July 17, 2026)
Yahoo Finance Live — "Nasdaq futures hammered by chips sell-off, Dow and S&P 500 futures fall" (July 17, 2026)
Netflix guidance disappoints even as the quarter itself was in line. Q2 EPS of $0.80 on $12.56B revenue roughly matched estimates, but forecasting a second straight quarter of slowing sales growth sent shares down more than 11% after hours Thursday — a reminder to clients that "beat the quarter" and "beat the stock reaction" aren't the same thing in this market.
Source: CNBC — Netflix (NFLX) Q2 2026 Earnings (July 16, 2026)
Ben Carlson: "The Mag 7 is Losing." The S&P 493 — everything outside the mega-cap seven — is responsible for roughly 96% of the index's price-only return year-to-date, with six of the seven Mag 7 names sitting in double-digit drawdowns. A clean talking point for clients who assume index performance still means "the same seven stocks are carrying everything."
Source: A Wealth of Common Sense — Ben Carlson, "The Mag 7 is Losing?" (July 5, 2026)

3. Fixed Income

10-year yield holding near 4.57-4.60%, curve still positively sloped. Full curve as of July 16 close: 3-month 3.79%, 1-yr 3.98%, 2-yr 4.16%, 5-yr 4.28%, 10-yr 4.57%, 30-yr 5.09%. The 10s-3mo spread is +78bp and 10s-2s is +41bp. The 10-year briefly touched a near-two-month high of 4.62% on July 13 before softer CPI data pulled it back.
Source: Trading Economics — US 10 Year Treasury Note Yield
Rate-hike odds are back on the table, whipsawed by both inflation data and oil. June CPI came in soft (headline +3.5% y/y vs. 3.8% expected), which knocked July-hike odds down to roughly 17% — but the Iran/Hormuz-driven oil spike pushed September-hike pricing from ~66% toward near-certainty as the 2-year yield touched a 16-month high of 4.24%. The July 28-29 FOMC meeting itself is expected to hold steady while keeping the door open to tightening later this year.
Source: CNBC — "Treasury yields slide after June CPI slows much more than expected" (July 14, 2026)
Wolf Street: the bond market is telling the Fed to get on with it. The 6-month Treasury yield has climbed back to 4% — a level that only makes sense if the market expects the Fed to hike, not cut, from here. A good short-duration data point for clients asking why cash and T-bill yields haven't come down despite talk of Fed rate cuts earlier this year.
Source: Wolf Street — "Six-Month Treasury Yield Rises to 4%" (July 4, 2026)

4. Geopolitical Events

The US escalated in the Strait of Hormuz — naval blockade plus a transit fee. Washington moved to enforce a maritime blockade on Iranian vessels and impose a 20% transit fee on other commercial cargo passing through the strait, which handles roughly 20% of world oil traffic. Brent crude jumped as much as 9.6% to $83.30/barrel on the news — its best single-day gain since 2020 — before easing slightly as the US said 8.5M+ barrels still transited safely with military escort on July 12.
Bloomberg Brief — "Oil Rises as US, Iran Dispute Whether Hormuz is Open; Big Week in Markets" (July 13, 2026)
Source: CNBC — "Brent oil jumps more than 9%...after Trump reinstates Iran blockade" (July 12, 2026)
A major tariff deadline hits July 24 — and the replacement is still being written. The 10% Section 122 emergency tariff (in effect since February 24) hits its 150-day statutory limit on July 24 and lapses without congressional action. USTR's proposed replacement — a 12.5% duty on imports from 46 countries including China, Japan, Vietnam, India, Thailand, and South Korea — is due July 20. Worth flagging to clients with import-exposed holdings: the rate could go up, down, or lapse depending on what's finalized in the next few days.
Source: Tariffs Tool — "Section 122 Expires July 24, 2026: Rates After + 3 Scenarios"
Japan's trade deal came in better than feared. The US-Japan agreement set auto tariffs at 15%, well below the 25% that had been floated — a relief that JPMorgan estimates could lift Japanese corporate earnings by roughly 3 percentage points, or GDP by 0.3 points year-over-year. Contrast this with Brazil, which was hit with a 50% tariff effective August 1, five times its original 10% Liberation Day rate — a reminder that trade-policy outcomes are diverging sharply by country right now.
Source: J.P. Morgan Global Research — "US Tariffs: What's the Impact?"
